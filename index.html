Case-law strip

Moffatt v. Air Canada

Your AI's words are your words; a chatbot's hallucination is the company's liability.

Thaler v. Perlmutter

No human author, no copyright; raw AI output may not be yours to own.

FTC v. Rite Aid

Fine-tune on data you can't cleanly delete, and a regulator can force you to destroy the model.

Soteria v. IBM (2022)

A delayed project plus no liability cap equals a "wasted costs" claim that can end a company.

Vernor v. Autodesk

"License" vs "sale" is decided by your contract's language — and it controls product-liability exposure.

Specht v. Netscape

Browsewrap without affirmative consent is unenforceable; consent design is everything.

A map of every AI risk protects no one on its own. It matters only once it becomes something a company can deploy — so I turned the doctrine into instruments.

CLOSE

Why I Care

I didn't arrive at legal tech from software. I arrived from a courtroom. Three and a half years of litigation showed me how much legal work is repeatable — and how much defensible work never reaches the people who need it, because it's too slow or too expensive. The legal tech I care about stays close to that: built around real client problems — contracts, compliance, privacy, AI, diligence, digital business risk — not automation for its own sake. I've spent eight months proving I can build it, alone. I'd rather build it inside a team already taking it seriously.

Models are routed by data sensitivity. Public-only diagnostics run on free fast-inference tiers — no privacy cost, the input is already public. Client and work data run on paid API tiers with model training disabled. Every demo here uses public or synthetic data only.

All work shown is mine; every link and demo is genuine.
