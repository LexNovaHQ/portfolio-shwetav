Act I · The legal problem

Legal diligence fails when the answer arrives before the facts are properly characterised.

The engine was built to solve a practice problem: technology businesses describe themselves through products and marketing language, while law attaches to specific conduct, roles, affected persons, assets, jurisdictions, controls and evidence.

01

The conventional shortcut

A target is labelled “AI”, “FinTech” or “SaaS”; a broad risk list is generated; and legal issues are presented without proving which product activity actually engages them.

02

The legal failure

Function, role, context, authority, trigger conditions, controls, exclusions and evidential limits collapse into one opaque conclusion that is difficult to review or defend.

03

The architecture I built

Every material conclusion is tied to a defined output, permitted evidence, factual conditions, forbidden inferences, an exclusion path, a limitation state and a downstream legal route.

The decision grammar

For each legal fact, the system asks:

  1. What exactly must be established?
  2. Which evidence is admissible for that conclusion?
  3. What factual conditions must be satisfied?
  4. What must disqualify or narrow the conclusion?
  5. Which inference is forbidden?
  6. What result can safely be stated?
  7. What remains unresolved?
  8. Where must the issue route next?

Legal guardrails

The system is designed not to overclaim.

A business label never proves a regulated activity.
A risk surface never proves a legal exposure.
A clause cannot cure a defective product or consent flow.
A technical control cannot cure defective legal allocation.
Missing public proof becomes a diligence question, not an accusation.

01

Decompose the offering into activities

The unit of analysis is not the company or the product label. It is the distinct activity, reconstructed through actors, inputs, actions, outputs, affected objects, autonomy and human control.

02

Route the obligations created by the classified activity

The taxonomy narrows the review universe. It does not itself decide applicability or compliance. It determines which obligations must be investigated and what evidence or control would answer them.

Act III · The legal method as a running system

The technology preserves the legal decision chain; it does not replace it.

The runtime is designed around the order in which a defensible legal review should happen: evidence before inference, mechanics before classification, classification before exposure, controls before final status, and human review before legal use.

EvidencePublic and supplied material is captured before analysis.
ProfilesTarget, activity, data and legal context are reconstructed.
ClassificationBehavior, lane and surface are resolved per activity.
RoutingOnly relevant obligations and exposure rows enter review.
ApplicationConditions, controls, exclusions and limits are tested.
HandoffFindings route to drafting, evidence and qualified review.

What the system can do

Structure and preserve legal work

  • Navigate lossless evidence through purpose-built source indexes.
  • Apply field-level derivation conditions and forbidden-inference rules.
  • Classify domain-specific activities through mounted package taxonomies.
  • Route and evaluate individual obligation and exposure rows.
  • Preserve controls, exclusions, limitations and reinvestigation requests.
  • Compile the result into a review-ready legal work product.

What remains legal judgment

Applicability, position and final advice

  • Whether a law applies to the complete, private factual record.
  • Which competing legal characterisation should be adopted.
  • Whether a control is legally sufficient or commercially acceptable.
  • Which drafting position, risk allocation or remedy should be negotiated.
  • Whether local law creates non-waivable duties or licensing consequences.
  • Whether the final work may be relied upon in a real matter.
